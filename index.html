Foundations & Strategy

TOPIC 01

Introduction to Marketing Foundations

Key takeaways
  • Kotler's definition: marketing = managing profitable customer relationships — create value for customers to capture value in return.
  • Marketing evolved across five eras: 1.0 product → 2.0 customer → 3.0 human → 4.0 digital → 5.0 technology for humanity.
  • A need is a basic requirement; a desire is the specific, culturally-shaped way to satisfy it.
  • Core strategy is STP: Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning, ending in a differential advantage.
  • A perceptual map shows how brands are perceived versus competitors on key attributes.
Full detail
What is marketing?
"…marketing is managing profitable customer relationships. The aim of marketing is to create value for customers in order to capture value from customers in return."Kotler & Armstrong (2014)

Marketing was not always relationship-focused. Earlier definitions were transactional: Denner (1971) framed it as the permanent analysis of demand plus the means to satisfy it profitably; Baker (1987) as a process of exchange for mutual benefit and satisfaction.

The evolution of marketing (1.0 → 5.0)
  • 1.0 — product-centric: focus on making and selling the product.
  • 2.0 — customer-centric: satisfy and retain the customer.
  • 3.0 — human-centric: values, emotions, the human spirit.
  • 4.0 — digital-centric: blends online & offline interaction.
  • 5.0 — technology for humanity: tech mimics human marketers to create value.
Needs vs. desire

A need is a basic requirement (hunger, safety). A desire/want is the specific, culturally-shaped way a person chooses to satisfy it. Marketing works largely on shaping and channeling desire.

STP — the strategic sequence

Segmentation divides a market into subsets with common needs (geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioural; in B2B by business units). A customer persona is a semi-fictional profile of a key segment. Positioning builds a differential advantage so the offer is preferred over competitors'.

The perceptual map

Displays how brands are perceived relative to each other on key attributes (e.g. price vs. quality) — useful to spot gaps and decide where to position.

TOPIC 02

Marketing 5.0 Foundations

Key takeaways
  • Marketing 5.0 = technology that mimics humans to create, deliver and enhance value across the journey.
  • Five components: 2 disciplines (Data-Driven, Agile) + 3 applications (Predictive, Contextual, Augmented).
  • IDFA (Identifier For Advertisers) + ATT (App Tracking Transparency) reshaped tracking around privacy & trust.
  • CTV (Connected TV) is a fast-growing channel; most ad spend has shifted to internet/digital.
Full detail
Benefits of Marketing 5.0
  • More informed decisions from big data.
  • Predict outcomes of strategies/tactics.
  • Bring the contextual digital experience into the physical world.
  • Augment frontline marketers' capacity.
  • Speed up execution.
The five components
TypeComponentMeaning
DisciplineData-DrivenUse big data to collect, analyse and optimise decisions.
DisciplineAgileDecentralised teams, fast deployment, high adaptability.
ApplicationPredictiveAnticipate outcomes (customer, product, brand).
ApplicationContextualPersonalised info, customised interaction, total immersion.
ApplicationAugmentedTiered interfaces + digital tools for frontliners.
Privacy: IDFA & ATT

IDFA is a per-device identifier enabling cross-device tracking. ATT is Apple's framework requiring user opt-in before tracking. Together they shift power around data monopoly, privacy and trust — tracking becomes trust-reliant.

The CTV avalanche

Connected TV blends the reach of TV with digital's targeting and measurement. Globally, internet/digital now takes the dominant share of forecast ad spend versus TV, outdoor, print and radio.

TOPIC 03

From Product to Brand Foundations

Key takeaways
  • Differentiate through four levers: experience, values, extended product, connection with the brand.
  • Move brands from awareness to relevance — relevance = connecting with desires, needs & aspirations.
  • Kotler's modern path is the 5 A's: Aware → Appeal → Ask → Act → Advocate.
  • Journeys are digital-dominated — in the automotive case, ~19 of ~24 touchpoints were digital.
  • Shift from transactions to relationships.
Full detail
Differentiation in the 21st century

Four levers: experience, values, extended product, connection with the brand. "The fastest route to a customer's heart is through their emotions."

  • Nike: runners' community, Nike+ Run Club & Training Club, Apple Watch — extended product + connection.
  • Apple "Today at Apple": experiential activities (photography, coding, editing) — differentiation via experience.
  • Starbucks: app/rewards, AI personalisation, Reserve Roasteries — personalised marketing + experience.
Levels of "experience" — points of impact

On the roadConventionsOffice visits Pop-up storesPhysical storesFairs (VR/AR) Street marketingMallsPoint-of-saleOnline

Pop-up stores get new eyes on the brand; fairs make a statement and involve the audience with VR/AR; street marketing pushes awareness → relevance.

From awareness to relevance

A brand is relevant when it connects with the desires, needs and aspirations of its audience — making lives easier and surprising them continuously. Everything relies on the relationship: fewer transactions, more relationships (acquisition costs, conversations, content).

The customer journey (5 A's)

In the classic automotive example, out of ~24 touchpoints, 19 were digital — so traditional and digital marketing must work together.

TOPIC 04

Storytelling & Content Marketing Foundations

Key takeaways
  • Storytelling = connecting with an audience through a story that has meaning to them.
  • The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell) is the recurring structure: known → unknown → abyss → transformation → return.
  • Build a story in 5 steps; the brand is context, not the protagonist; characters showcase its values.
  • The more vulnerable the character, the closer the audience feels.
  • Modern cases: Liquid Death (entertainment), Ryanair (radical transparency), Burger King (telling without telling).
Full detail
What is storytelling?

Connecting with an audience, using any medium (language, words, images, video, audio), through a story that has meaning to them. Forms evolved: Spoken → Visual → Written → Audiovisual → Interactive.

All great stories share: SettingCharactersPlotConflictThemeNarrative arc

The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell)
Building a brand story — 5 steps
  1. Define who to connect with — know their thoughts, fears, desires, needs at the moment of the message.
  2. Ideas & values to convey — values relate to fidelity; connect message → details → consequence (Ben & Jerry's climate strike).
  3. Emotions — empathise via a concern, need or fear; resolve so the brand association is positive (UNICEF).
  4. Narrative structure.
  5. Characters — something happens to them; the brand is context, not the hero; characters showcase brand values.

Vulnerability principle: the more vulnerable a character seems, the closer the audience feels — bonding with authentic, struggling characters around love, hope, fear, adventure and effort.

Types of stories
TypeIdea
FictionMade-up characters/situations.
HistoricalExperience, reputation, nostalgia.
Real-life situationsUsers see themselves in the use cases.
Based on brand valuesAudience links brand to characters' values.
"Who am I?"Intro story about the brand's history.
Telling without tellingImplied narrative & reaction (Burger King).
Modern cases
  • Liquid Death — entertainment: water is a boring commodity, so market "murdering your thirst", not "hydration" (the boring-product paradox).
  • Ryanair — radical transparency: embrace the villain arc; agree with haters and roast back; zero production value; admitting they're cheap builds trust.
  • Burger King / Wendy's: playful reactive social presence ("hamberders").

TOPIC 05

Mobile Marketing Digital

Key takeaways
  • MMA: engage audiences interactively through and with any mobile device or network.
  • Push = brand-initiated; Pull = user-initiated.
  • Formats: SMS/MMS (~98% open), QR (offline→online), push notifications, geofencing.
  • Apps act as loyalty tool, distribution channel and revenue source; in-app converts ~+130% vs mobile browser.
  • ASO improves store visibility; key challenges include ~28% quick uninstalls and privacy rules.
Full detail
"Mobile Marketing is a set of practices that enables organizations to communicate and engage with their audience in an interactive and relevant manner through and with any mobile device or network."Mobile Marketing Association (2020)
Key facts

60%+ of global web traffic is mobile; users spend 4+ hours daily on devices; channels include apps, SMS, QR codes and in-app ads.

Push vs. pull
PushPull
Brand sends the message (push notifications, SMS).User requests it (search, scan a QR, open an app).
Benefits & formats

Benefits: personalisation & segmentation, immediate response, interactivity, engaging experiences. Formats: SMS/MMS (~98% open), QR (offline-to-online bridge), push notifications, geofencing (location targeting); plus web banners, mobile web, AR, VR, m-commerce, apps. Cons: small screen, technical limits, constrained GUI.

Segmentation & tracking

Segment by: OS, device, screen size, features, location, enriched data. Track via: clicks/taps, geolocation, SMS/IM, app interaction, cross-site tracking.

Apps in marketing

An app is a loyalty tool, a distribution channel and a revenue source. Value: personalisation at scale, loyalty & gamification, first-party data, differentiation/positioning, a controlled environment at the purchase decision, two-way communication.

ASO & challenges

ASO (App Store Optimization) improves visibility/ranking via downloads, installs, engagement, reviews. Challenges: high uninstall rates (~28% uninstalled quickly), privacy regulation (GDPR, iOS), UX/UI issues, platform restrictions. Cases: IKEA (AR) and Sephora (app + in-store).

TOPIC 06

SEO & SEM Digital

Key takeaways
  • SEO = organic traffic via search results (MOZ); engines work Crawl → Index → Rank.
  • Three search intents: informational, transactional, navigational — each with different SERP features.
  • Search is shifting to GEO (cited by AI answers) and AEO (direct answers); strengthen E-E-A-T.
  • Long-tail keywords carry ~70% of traffic; fat head ~18.5%, chunky middle ~11.5%.
  • SEM = paid search visibility; SEO = organic. Avoid black-hat (cloaking, keyword stuffing).
Full detail
What is SEO?
Search Engine Optimization is the practice of increasing the quantity and quality of traffic to your website through organic search engine results.MOZ (2018)

Building blocks: keywords, meta tags, content, link building, speed, HTTPS, mobile & voice. Engines Crawl → Index → Rank and surface SERP features (videos, images). Discovery uses external links (follow/no-follow), Search Console, robots.txt, sitemap.xml.

Search intent
IntentExampleTypical SERP
Informational"What is the Metaverse?"AI Overview, Featured Snippets, Knowledge Graph, PAA, organic.
Transactional"Nike running shoes on sale"Shopping Ads, Popular Products, organic.
Navigational"Zara store Barcelona"Local Map Pack, PAA, reviews, video carousel.
The future: SEO → GEO → AEO

By 2026 ~30% of searches will occur through AI engines (Gartner, 2024). GEO (Generative Engine Optimization): being cited by AI answers (Query → LLM retrieval/RAG → summarization → response → citation). AEO (Answer Engine Optimization): optimising for direct answers (snippets, FAQ schema, voice).

Make AI choose you: structured data (schema, FAQs, JSON-LD); clear factual answers; strengthen E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust); unique data-backed insights; LLM-friendly site.

Keywords & the search demand curve

Choose keywords by search volume but don't overuse. Tools: Google Keyword Planner, Google Trends, AnswerThePublic, MOZ Keyword Explorer, SEMRush.

On-site, technical SEO & linkbuilding
  • On-site: title 50–60 chars, descriptions 150–300 chars, headers (h1/h2/h3), internal links, avoid duplicate content; image optimisation (weight, alt, speed, "-" in URLs); avoid chain redirects.
  • Technical: domain/hosting, structured data, canonical URLs, responsive design, AMP, SRCSET & lazy loading, multilingual URLs, HTTPS. Black-hat: cloaking, keyword stuffing.
  • Linkbuilding: inbound links judged on quality, diversity, similar topics; clean anchor text; E-A-T.
KPIs & SEM

KPIs: engagement, conversion rate, time on page, bounce rate, scroll depth, traffic source, CTR, backlinks, speed. SEM = paid search ads; SEO = organic. SEM sits within paid media (online ads, social, influencer, audience networks).

TOPIC 07

Digital Advertising Digital

Key takeaways
  • Banner types: trap, impulsive, retention; performance depends on CTR, visibility, layout, size, frequency.
  • Video ad formats: skippable, non-skippable, discovery, bumper (≤6s), outstream.
  • Programmatic/RTB: user → SSP → DSP bids → highest bid wins → ad served.
  • Retargeting follows the user across display, social and email.
  • Social ads structure: Campaign → Ad Set → Ad; objectives map to CPM/CPC/CPA by funnel stage.
Full detail
Formats

Banners (standard size/location): types are trap (clickbait), impulsive, retention; performance depends on CTR, visibility, content, layout, animations, logos, size ("real estate"), rotation/frequency. "Megabanners" run on sites and social networks for notoriety. Sponsorships link a brand to a recognised organisation.

Pop-ups / intrusive: InterstitialSkyscraperWebsite takeoverButtonFull pageExpandable — full-page takes the whole screen, is closable by a button, annoys users, but is not penalised by search engines.

Video ads (YouTube/Google): skippable, non-skippable, discovery, bumper (≤6s), outstream. Quality depends on content/video quality, formats, CTA/description and goals.

Programmatic & RTB

Players: advertisers/publishers, ad networks, ad exchanges, DSPs (advertisers bid), SSPs (publishers offer inventory), trading desks, data providers, ad servers, DMP/Ad Exchange (manages data, where purchase happens). RTB benefits: reduces costs, guides strategy, protects brand, unifies channels, budget control, better targeting. Campaign types: Prospecting · Targeting · Retargeting.

Remarketing / retargeting

Search retargeting; display ads (personalised, across the web); social media ads; email marketing (based on prior interactions).

Social media advertising
Paid socialOrganic
Paid & targeted, faster results, measurable/optimisable (ROAS, CPC, CTR, A/B).Earned & limited reach, long-term engagement, limited control.

Account structure: Campaign (objective) → Ad Set (audience, budget, schedule, placements) → Ad (creative).

Funnel stageObjectiveMetric
AwarenessReach, impressionsCPM
ConsiderationTraffic, engagement, video viewsCPC, CPV

Targeting: location, demographics, behaviour, connections, interests; Custom Audiences (lists, app users, site visitors); Lookalike Audiences. Bidding: CPC/CPM/CPA; budget daily vs lifetime; always A/B test.

TOPIC 08

Personal Branding Digital

Key takeaways
  • A personal brand is the perception of you; personal branding is the conscious effort to shape it.
  • It does three things: market yourself · convey a message · differentiate.
  • The model has many areas (Direction, Persona, Market, Product, Brand, Image, Communication…) each with guiding questions.
  • Networking is the No. 1 cause of job attainment; an elevator pitch is 30–90s, 90–270 words, 8–24 sentences.
  • Use SWOT and brand archetypes; avoid the 7 capital sins.
Full detail
Personal brand vs personal branding

A personal brand is a widely-recognised perception of an individual based on experience, expertise, competencies, actions and achievements. Personal branding is the conscious, intentional effort to create and influence that perception — positioning the person as an authority, elevating credibility, differentiating from competition. It does three things: market yourself, convey a message, differentiate.

What we look for: CredibilityPresencePositioningVisibilityJob hunting — through consistent personality & values, pull marketing (content), and online reputation monitoring.

Networking & the elevator pitch
"The exchange of information or services among individuals, groups, or institutions… the cultivation of productive relationships for employment or business."Merriam-Webster (2019)

Networking remains the No. 1 cause of job attainment (Lancaster, 2015). The elevator pitch is ~30–90 seconds, ~90–270 words, 8–24 sentences, covering: about you, what you offer, benefits. Answer: who are you? what do you do? how? what do you need? how to keep in touch?

SWOT
Values, archetypes & reputation

Keep in mind: Patience · Planning · Responsibility · Perseverance · Passion. Personality can be framed with brand archetypes (Caregiver, Ruler, Sage, Explorer, Innocent, Creator, Outlaw, Magician, Everyman…), each tied to a core drive. ORM monitors: what they say, how, where, who, and the intention.

7 capital sins of personal branding: Falsehood · Demotivation · Inconsistency · Selfishness · Vanity · Staggering · Reckless ambition.

TOPIC 09

Advergaming & Inbound Marketing Digital

Key takeaways
  • Inbound attracts with valuable content; outbound interrupts with unwanted messages (HubSpot).
  • Inbound methodology: Attract → Convert → Close → Delight.
  • Lead lifecycle: Generation → Qualification → Nurturing → Scoring → Distribution → CRM.
  • Qualification frameworks: BANT, MEDDIC, CHAMP, ANUM, FAINT, GPCTBA/C&I; scoring is explicit (A/B/C) × implicit (1/2/3).
  • Advergaming = advertising + entertainment; the flow state matches challenge to skill.
Full detail
Inbound vs. outbound
Inbound marketing attracts customers by creating valuable content and experiences tailored to them. While outbound interrupts your audience with content they don't want, inbound forms connections they're looking for and solves problems they already have.HubSpot (2020)
The inbound methodology
  • Attract: content, whitepapers, e-books, templates, demos, freebies.
  • Convert: forms, newsletters, CTAs, landing pages.
  • Close: lead management, emails, CRM.
  • Delight: personalised offers, smart content, CRM.
Lifecycle of a lead

Generation → Qualification → Nurturing → Dynamic Scoring → Distribution → CRM. Lead magnets are gated content (e-books, demos, discounts, courses) exchanged for prospect info.

Lead qualification

SAL (Sales Accepted Lead) → MQL/SQL. Frameworks:

FrameworkStands for
BANTBudget · Authority · Need · Time
MEDDICMetrics · Economic buyer · Decision criteria · Decision process · Identify pain · Champion
CHAMPChallenges · Authority · Money · Prioritization
ANUMAuthority · Need · Urgency · Money
FAINTFunds · Authority · Interest · Need · Timing
Dynamic scoring
  • Explicit: directly shared data (job, income, location, age) via forms. Scale A, B, C.
  • Implicit: monitored behaviour (opened mails, clicks, forms, calls, contests). Scale 1, 2, 3.

Combined, e.g. A1 = high explicit + high implicit → hot lead. Distribution: ColdWarmHot → CRM outreach (Prospecting → Approach → Needs → Presentation → Close → Follow-up).

Advergaming & the flow state

Advergaming = advertising + entertainment through (usually short) games. Spectrum: CasualAdvergamesVideo gamesMMOGsMMORPGsVirtual realities. Terms: avatar, community, currency, emoticons, grid, latency, universe.

Games drive positive experiences and engagement via freedom of choice, competition, meaning and progress. Examples: Roblox × Gucci, Fortnite, M&M's. They raise brand awareness/notoriety, teach, and dynamise digital strategy. (Jane McGonigal's TED talk: "playing is positive.")
